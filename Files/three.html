Overview

Class 3 marks a significant transition from basic literacy to structured academic learning. In Nepali, students progress from simple sentences to composing 5-6 sentence essays on topics like "मेरो घर" using descriptive vocabulary and proper grammar (क्रिया, विशेषण). English lessons introduce tenses (simple present/past) and guided reading of moral stories, fostering comprehension and spoken fluency. Mathematics shifts from counting to practical applications—mastering multiplication tables (1-10), solving fraction-based word problems (e.g., sharing food equally), and measuring objects in centimeters. These skills are reinforced through real-life scenarios, such as calculating the cost of stationery in mock market activities.

The Science curriculum sparks curiosity through hands-on experiments, like observing the water cycle by boiling water or charting plant growth in classroom gardens. Students learn to classify living organisms (e.g., parts of a flower) and explore states of matter (solid, liquid, gas) using local examples (ice from the Himalayas, water from Bagmati River). Social Studies expands their worldview, linking local governance (roles of village councils) to national identity (Nepal’s provinces, historical figures like Prithvi Narayan Shah). Map-reading exercises using Nepal’s geography build spatial awareness, while storytelling sessions highlight cultural festivals like Dashain and Tihar.

Teachers employ activity-based learning to cater to diverse styles. For example: Math: Fraction puzzles with paper pizzas. Science: "Rain in a Jar" experiments to simulate the water cycle. Language: Role-playing characters from Nepali folktales. Weekly 30-minute computer lab sessions bridge theory and practice, while collaborative projects (e.g., designing a "Clean School" poster) build critical thinking and civic responsibility.

Progress is tracked through formative tools like spelling bees, timed multiplication drills, and oral storytelling assessments. Summative evaluations include term-end projects, such as creating a "Pollution-Free Village" model using recycled materials. Parents are engaged via: Portfolio Sharing: Showcasing essays, art, and science reports. Participation Events: Inviting families to cultural performances or science exhibitions.

By year-end, students demonstrate competency in writing structured paragraphs, solving two-step math problems, and explaining scientific phenomena (e.g., "Why does ice melt?"). The curriculum ensures a smooth transition to Class 4’s advanced topics (e.g., division, Nepali grammar rules, and coding basics in ICT).

Academic Tracks

The CDC (Curriculum Development Centre) in Nepal's Class 3 curriculum focuses on developing foundational literacy and life skills, integrating different learning areas, and fostering an interest in arts and aesthetics. It's competency-based, soft skills-integrated, and emphasizes a learner-centered approach. The curriculum is structured around themes, with integrated lessons across subjects.

Cirriculum

Class 3 introduces advanced concepts like multiplication, essays, and the water cycle. ICT is added to foster digital skills. Competencies include independent reading, solving fraction problems, and using computers for simple tasks.

Based on the above context, the following competencies have been determined for early childhood development and education:

(A) Competence

  • Read short stories fluently.
  • Multiply/divide numbers (1–10).
  • Identify Nepal’s national symbols.
