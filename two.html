Overview

Class 2 builds on the foundational skills acquired in Class 1, with a stronger emphasis on language fluency and problem-solving. Nepali and English lessons now include sentence formation, basic grammar, and reading short paragraphs. Mathematics expands to addition/subtraction (up to 100), time-telling, and money calculations, using real-life scenarios like mock markets to teach practical skills.

Science topics explore seasons, food groups, and health habits, linking theory to daily life (e.g., "Why we eat balanced meals"). Social Studies delves deeper into community roles, local occupations, and Nepalese traditions, fostering civic pride. Creative Arts shifts from free drawing to guided storytelling and dance, encouraging self-expression.

The daily schedule introduces slightly longer periods (30‚Äì45 minutes) to accommodate growing attention spans. Competencies include writing 3‚Äì4 sentence paragraphs, solving word problems, and describing local festivals‚Äîall assessed through quizzes, projects, and oral presentations. Teachers use flashcards, group discussions, and simple experiments to cater to diverse learning styles.

A highlight of Class 2 is the introduction of peer learning, where students collaborate on projects like "Our Community" posters. By year-end, learners should read age-appropriate books independently, demonstrate basic arithmetic accuracy, and articulate their thoughts clearly‚Äîa stepping stone to more complex concepts in Class 3.

Academic Tracks

The CDC (Curriculum Development Centre) in Nepal's Grade 2 curriculum, part of the Basic Level (Grades 1-3), uses an integrated approach, incorporating subject-specific themes and competencies. It emphasizes developing individuals' potential, fostering national values, and preparing them for a modern world while also focusing on social unity and environmental conservation. The curriculum is learner-centered, encouraging active participation and responsibility, and uses a combination of textbooks and workbooks.

Cirriculum

Class 2 builds on foundational skills with structured lessons in language and math. Students learn to write short paragraphs, solve arithmetic problems, and understand community roles. Interactive methods (role-play, experiments) are used to teach science and social studies.
